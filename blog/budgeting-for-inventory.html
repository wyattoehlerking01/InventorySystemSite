How to Budget for School Inventory Next Year

Mar 21, 2026 • 8 min read

Every spring, administrators face the same challenge: how much should we budget for supplies next year? Most resort to this formula:

"Let's use last year's number, maybe add 5-10% for inflation."

It's a guess. And guesses leave money on the table or create shortages.

The problem with "same as last year"

Schools aren't static. Student populations change. New programs launch. Electives shift. What you spent on art supplies three years ago has nothing to do with what you'll spend this year.

Guessing also means you either under-budget (and face shortages mid-year) or over-budget (and tie up money that could fund other priorities).

A better approach: data-driven budgeting

Here's what schools with real inventory tracking do differently:

1. Analyze your historical spend by category

Pull reports on what you actually spent: lab supplies vs. art materials vs. office supplies vs. athletics equipment. Look at trends over 2-3 years. You'll likely see patterns.

Example: "We spend $12,500/year on science lab supplies. Art program spends $4,200. Athletics: $8,900."

2. Account for known changes

A new section of chemistry being added? That adds ~20% to science supplies. A freshman class that's grown larger than expected? More textbooks and consumables needed. Budget for the reality of next year, not the average of the past.

3. Calculate per-unit costs and utilization

How many Bunsen burners per student? Lab notebooks per section? Safety goggles per class? If you know the per-unit cost and enrollment, the budget math becomes straightforward.

4. Include seasonal / one-time items

Some supplies are annual but forgotten in budgeting: seasonal athletic gear, once-a-year field day materials, graduation-related supplies. Build these in explicitly.

5. Add buffer for waste, loss, and emergencies

In reality, not everything lasts the full year. Some items break. Some get lost. Add 5-10% contingency.

A real example

One high school we worked with tracked this for science department:

  • Chemistry: 3 sections × 30 students = 90 students. Per-student lab supply cost: $85/year. Total: $7,650
  • Biology: 5 sections × 25 students = 125 students. Per-student cost: $40/year. Total: $5,000
  • Physics: 2 sections × 20 students = 40 students. Per-student cost: $120/year. Total: $4,800
  • Equipment replacement: $2,000/year (recurring)
  • Contingency (10%): $1,945

Total science budget: $21,395

Previously, they budgeted $20,000 based on "it's roughly what we spent before." The data-driven approach revealed they were under-budgeting by $1,400 per year—creating preventable shortages.

Tools that make this easier

If you have real inventory tracking (not just spreadsheets), generating these reports is minutes instead of hours. You can answer: "What did we spend on category X last year?" vs. reconstructing it from fragmented data.

Bottom line

Budget based on what you'll actually need, not on past guesses. Use data. Account for changes. Build in buffer. The result is more predictable spending, fewer mid-year crunches, and money available for priorities that matter.
