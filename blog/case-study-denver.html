CASE STUDY

How Denver Schools Cut Supply Costs by 28%

A real district, real savings, real impact

Mar 14, 2026 • 7 min read

Denver Public Schools manages 67 schools across primary, middle, and high school grades. Operating budgets are tight. Superintendent Maria Rodriguez knew they weren't managing inventory well.

"We had supply budgets spread across schools, but no visibility into what was actually being spent," she told us recently.

The result? Inefficiency. Duplicate purchases. Waste. Last year, DPS spent $2.8M on supplies district-wide. She suspected they could do better.

The challenge

Each school managed its own supplies independently. They used spreadsheets, emails, and handshake agreements to coordinate. When art supplies ran low at Lincoln High, they'd order instead of checking if they had stock at Washington Middle 5 miles away.

Key problems:

  • No visibility across district: "Do we have those safety goggles in stock anywhere?"
  • No bulk purchasing discounts: Schools ordered individually instead of collectively
  • Duplicate spending: Different schools buying the same items at different prices
  • Waste & loss: No tracking meant lost items never got replaced efficiently
  • Budget surprises: Overages discovered mid-year instead of prevented

The solution

DPS implemented a centralized inventory system with real-time visibility across all 67 schools. Each location could see not just their own inventory, but stock at every other school.

Key changes:

  • Centralized visibility: Supply coordinators could search district-wide: "I need 100 lab notebooks. Do we have them?"
  • Smart reordering: System flagged when items were low across the district, prompting bulk buys instead of 67 individual orders
  • Transfer instead of order: If one school had excess while another was short, staff could initiate transfers instead of new purchases
  • Spend tracking: Real-time dashboards showed which categories schools were overspending in, informing next year's budgets

Results

After six months of full implementation, the numbers were clear:

  • $784,000 in savings (28% reduction from baseline)
  • 42% fewer duplicate purchases – bulk buying was coordinated district-wide
  • Waste reduction of 19% – better tracking meant items weren't lost; they knew what they had
  • Supply coordinators freed up 200+ hours annually from manual tracking
  • Zero mid-year budget overages – visibility meant budgets stayed on track

What they reinvested the savings in

Here's where the impact gets real. DPS didn't pocket the savings. They reinvested:

  • $250K into teacher professional development programs
  • $180K for additional lab equipment and tech resources
  • $200K to fund 3 new full-time custodial positions (reducing workload)
  • $154K for student enrichment supplies (arts, music, STEM)

"The savings weren't the goal," Superintendent Rodriguez reflected. "The goal was to free up money for things that directly help students learn. This system did that."

The ripple effects

Beyond the budget line item, the system changed how the district operates:

Teachers have better supplies. When inventory was invisible, shortages happened silently. Now they're prevented. Teachers show up to class and materials are in stock.

Coordinators work smarter. Instead of 3 hours a week updating spreadsheets, they spend time solving real problems. Supply coordinator James Chen: "I'm actually managing inventory now, not just updating Excel."

Budgets are predictable. Next year's budget isn't a guess. It's based on data about what was actually used and what's needed.

What about other districts?

Denver's success isn't unique. The problems they solved are universal: waste, duplication, poor visibility, coordinator burnout.

Any district can get similar results if they:

  1. Get visibility into what they actually have and spend
  2. Coordinate purchasing across schools instead of ordering individually
  3. Track usage to forecast future needs accurately
  4. Free coordinators up for strategic work instead of data entry

For Denver, the math is simple: 28% savings means millions of dollars back to students. That's worth solving.
